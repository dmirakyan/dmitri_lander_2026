On Travel

I firmly believe that anyone, given the opportunity, should spend a bit of time being nomadic.

To those who fully embrace it, the experience looks a bit like defying gravity. Don’t like winter? Never have to experience one again.

Each weekend feels like a small vacation. Fall in love with a place, and you can extend your stay for a month, then maybe three more. To the dopamine-seeking brain, this is particularly delightful. There’s no shortage of sensations - sights, smells, colors. Whether that’s food or scenery or people you meet along the way. Reach out and it’s there for you.

There’s something particularly vivid about the experience of traveling solo. The complete and utter freedom to move slowly and explore, without any compromises or concerns. The preferred dosage for solo travel varies from person to person, but the feeling of freedom after you do it once sticks with you for a while.

Speaking of people. The (unsurprising) reality is that you remember the faces much more than the sights. The late-night conversations on rooftops or around campfires. In traveler’s hostels, there’s a perpetual feeling of freshman year of school. Everyone’s curious and eager to make friends, and nobody knows who you are or what to expect from you.

In aggregate, it breeds a certain level of universal empathy. No matter where you’re from or who you are, we all have the same worries. On the margin, it gives you an appreciation for the distinctness of certain cultures and broadens the palette of who you choose to be. The overwhelming warmth of Argentina. The care to detail of Japan. The anxiety-fueled hustle of NYC.

There’s a certain desire to pick and choose traits of different places you visit and somehow weave them into your own cloth. I can’t say I’ve succeeded at this, but I like the feeling that your identity is a choice.

The things people assume about travel (both good and bad) turn out to be mostly false. Routines are trivial to maintain if you commit to staying in any one place for at least a month. The majority of the world has great wifi. There’s a decent chance wherever you go is far more safe than the Bay or NYC. And you can find a community among other travelers as quickly as you would in any major city back home.

The flip-side to this is that packing your bags and going abroad probably won’t bring any grand epiphanies. It won’t fill any holes or offer up any mystical revelations. You’ll come back 3 months later looking a bit tanner, but feeling no less lost. The bigger knots, it turns out, only get untangled through effort and time. At best, the adventures become a distraction. The line between self-discovery and escapism starts to blur.

With time, the best thing travel did for me was give me more to draw from. More palettes for what life can look like. More examples of people living differently than how I grew up. And the reminder that large parts of the world are just… pretty great.
